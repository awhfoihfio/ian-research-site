## Thesis

The nuclear supply chain is becoming strategically important again as electricity demand rises, AI data centers require more stable power, and the U.S. government pushes to reduce dependence on foreign nuclear fuel supply. Within this theme, Energy Fuels (UUUU) and Centrus Energy (LEU) offer two different types of exposure.

UUUU is more exposed to domestic uranium mining and processing. LEU is more exposed to uranium enrichment, low-enriched uranium (LEU), and future HALEU demand. Both companies benefit from the same broad theme, but their risks are very different.

My current view is that UUUU is a domestic supply-chain scarcity asset, while LEU is a higher-upside but more execution-sensitive enrichment story.

## UUUU: Domestic Uranium Processing Exposure

Energy Fuels owns the White Mesa Mill, which the company describes as the only fully licensed and operating conventional uranium mill in the United States. This makes UUUU difficult to replace in the domestic uranium processing chain, especially if U.S. policy continues to emphasize nuclear fuel security and domestic critical mineral capacity. [Energy Fuels](https://www.energyfuels.com/white-mesa-mill/)

The U.S. is not one of the dominant global uranium producers. Kazakhstan and Canada remain much more important in global uranium production. However, the key point for UUUU is not global production scale. The key point is domestic strategic relevance. If the U.S. wants more control over its nuclear fuel supply chain, UUUU's processing infrastructure becomes more valuable.

AI data centers also strengthen the long-term nuclear thesis. Data centers need large-scale, stable electricity. Nuclear power is one of the few sources that can provide firm, low-carbon baseload power at scale. This does not mean UUUU directly benefits from every data center buildout, but it does support the broader nuclear fuel cycle thesis.

## UUUU Risks

The biggest risk is that UUUU's value depends heavily on U.S. policy support for domestic uranium and nuclear fuel security. If policy attention fades, the scarcity value of domestic processing capacity may be discounted by the market.

UUUU also does not have the same mining and processing scale as global leaders such as Cameco or Kazatomprom. Its strategic importance is high, but its operating scale is still limited. In addition, UUUU does not solve the entire nuclear fuel chain. Enrichment remains a separate bottleneck, and that market is still influenced by Russia and China.

The company may also require continued capital investment before the full domestic supply-chain story is reflected in earnings. This makes timing important. The thesis may be right, but cash flow realization could take longer than expected.

## LEU: Enrichment and HALEU Exposure

Centrus Energy is a more direct play on enrichment and advanced nuclear fuel. LEU has exposure to low-enriched uranium and HALEU, which are critical inputs for the future nuclear fuel cycle, especially if advanced reactors and small modular reactors become commercially meaningful.

Centrus reported a total backlog of $3.9 billion as of March 31, 2026, extending to 2040. However, a significant portion of the LEU backlog is contingent on future production capacity and the ability to secure substantial public and private investment. [Centrus Energy](https://investors.centrusenergy.com/news-releases/news-release-details/centrus-reports-first-quarter-2026-results)

This is the core attraction and the core risk. LEU has a strong strategic position, but investors need to separate contracted demand from near-term revenue conversion.

## LEU Risks

The biggest risk for LEU is timing. Advanced nuclear and SMR deployment may take longer than expected because of regulation, financing, and commercialization delays. If major SMR deployments are pushed toward the end of the decade, LEU could face a multi-year gap between market expectations and realized revenue.

There is also Russia-related supply-chain risk. The U.S. has banned imports of Russian low-enriched uranium, but the Department of Energy allows waivers under certain conditions to avoid disruption to nuclear operators. [U.S. Department of Energy](https://www.energy.gov/ne/russian-uranium-ban-waiver-guidance)

This means LEU is not free from geopolitical risk. If Russia restricts supply earlier than expected, or if U.S. waiver policy becomes stricter before domestic capacity is ready, LEU's current commercial business could face pressure. On the other hand, if U.S.-Russia relations improve materially, the market may reduce the scarcity premium attached to domestic enrichment capacity.

## Fundamentals and Valuation

For UUUU, the market value and enterprise value have risen significantly from 2025 to 2026, while revenue remains unstable. This is understandable for a mining and processing company, but it also means valuation is being driven more by strategic optionality than by stable current earnings. Net income and EPS remain negative, which is consistent with a company still waiting for stronger operating leverage.

For LEU, the market has started to discount the difficulty of scaling enrichment capacity. Revenue and gross profit may appear relatively stable, but margins can be pressured by hiring, capital spending, and factory buildout costs. This creates a financial dichotomy: the long-term strategic position looks strong, while short-term earnings may look weaker during the scale-up phase.

## Current View

UUUU is a hold / watchlist position for me. I would not add aggressively at current levels unless the market gives a better entry point or there is stronger evidence that U.S. domestic uranium processing will receive sustained policy support.

LEU has higher upside if enrichment and HALEU capacity scale successfully, but it also carries more execution and timing risk. I would consider adding only during volatility, while closely watching U.S. government policy, Russian uranium waiver decisions, and the commercialization timeline of SMRs.

Overall, I prefer to treat both stocks as policy-linked nuclear supply-chain exposures rather than simple commodity trades.
